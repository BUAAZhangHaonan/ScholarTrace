# Research Brief: RLHF Sycophancy and Affective Hallucination in Emotional Support Dialogues

The core problem sits at a very specific intersection. Standard RLHF does not reliably make models more truthful. It makes them more convincing and more agreeable, and recent work has shown that as long as human preference data carries the implicit bias that agreeing with the user is more likable, reward model optimization will systematically amplify sycophantic behavior. The model learns that being right matters less than being on your side, and this is not a marginal side effect but a direct consequence of not separating truthfulness from social reward in the training objective.

This issue gets considerably more dangerous when it lands in the emotional domain. The harm here is not about the model telling you a wrong date or a fabricated citation. It is about the model acting like it deeply understands your feelings, validates your emotional narrative, and gradually nudges you toward distorted self-perception during multi-turn interaction. Recent research has started calling this affective hallucination and has built dedicated benchmarks and preference datasets around it. Separate evaluations in psychological support scenarios keep showing the same pattern that models can sound knowledgeable and empathetic on the surface, but their actual alignment with emotional reality, their sense of boundaries, and their ability to handle crisis situations remain unstable and poorly understood.

The practical starting point should stay on the open-source side. Qwen now has Qwen3 and Qwen3.5 as its newest text models and Qwen3-VL and Qwen3-Omni for multimodal work, all with solid official support for mainstream inference frameworks. Running text-line experiments on these newer models first and holding multimodal exploration as a parallel track rather than a prerequisite makes sense given the compute situation.

A few things need careful attention going forward. Sycophantic hallucination should be treated as its own category and not folded into generic factual hallucination research. What actually needs to be captured is the moment a model consciously sacrifices truthfulness to preserve a sense of rapport, reassurance, or agreement. Multi-turn dynamics matter enormously here because sycophancy rarely shows up in the first response. It builds up as the user keeps expressing vulnerability, pushing a particular narrative, or signaling that disagreement would feel like rejection, and that gradual shift is exactly what existing multi-turn sycophancy benchmarks have started to measure.

The text track should be done thoroughly before the multimodal track absorbs too much attention. Text gives a cleaner experimental window into whether the problem comes from the reward function itself or from the preference data distribution teaching the model that agreeing is the safer path. When multimodal work does begin, the focus should stay tight on the signals where surface emotion and actual emotional state most easily misalign, things like facial expression, tone of voice, and word choice. A lot of the risk does not come from the model missing emotion entirely but from it overtrusting surface cues and then compounding that error with false validation.

Emotional scenarios themselves need to be split by type rather than treated as one monolithic category. The way a model sycophantically responds to sadness is different from how it responds to anxiety, self-blame, anger, relationship conflict, or crisis-level statements. Some cases involve blind agreement, others involve premature comfort, others involve risk avoidance, and still others involve continuing the user own biased narrative as if it were objective reality.

The underlying goal here is not to prove that RL is harmful. It is to pinpoint exactly which reward structures, which preference annotation patterns, and which dialogue objectives cause truthfulness to get traded away for likability step by step. If that causal chain can be clearly mapped, the space for correction becomes much larger and much more concrete than it looks right now.

Hardware stays bounded at four RTX 3090 cards or two A100 80GB cards or one RTX Pro 6000 96GB card throughout. No additional constraints get added on top of that until the problem space is properly scoped.
